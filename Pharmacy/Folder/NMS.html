New Medicine Service

What is the NMS scheme?

People are often having issues when beginning a new medication, as part of the scheme, our pharmacist can support you over the course of several weeks to help you take the medication safely and as effectively as possible.

This service is only available to people using certain medications, in some cases, where there's a problem and a solution is not avaible, you'll be referred back to your doctor.

If you wish to join, you can speak to the pharmacist when you first start taking your medication, and ask any questions you may have about it.

How does the New Medicines Service work?


When you first start taking your medication, you can sit in our private consultation room with the pharmacist to ask about a wide variety of things such as side effects, or how to fit your treatment around your lifestyle. These appointments will take roughly 10 - 15 minutes, are completely free and will always be from the privacy of our consultation room.

You'lll then have a second appointment roughly two weeks later, where you and the pharmacist can speak about any issues you may be experiencing. For example, if you're finding it hard to take the medication regularly, or are having difficulties swallowing the tablets you have been perscribed. The pharmacist can work with you to come up with solutions and help you get back on track.

Your third and last appointment will take place another two weeks later. During this appointment you can catch up with the pharmacist again to see how your treatment is coming along. The service then ends, but our pharmacists will always be happy to talk to you about your medications when or if you ever need any help.

Who is elligible?

You are elligible for the New Medicine Service if you are recieving treatment for any of the following:
  • Asthma
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
  • Type 2 Diabetes
  • High Blood Pressure
  • High Cholesterol
  • Osteoporosis
  • Gout
  • Glaucoma
  • Epilepsy
  • Parkinson's Disease
  • Urinary Incontinence or Retention
  • Heart Failure
  • Coronary Heart Disease
  • Atrial Fibrillation
  • Unstable Angina or you have suffered from a heart attack
  • If you have suffered from a stroke or a Transient Ischaemic Attack
  • If you have a long-term risk of blood clots or blocked blood vessels, including DVT (Deep Vein Thrombosis)
